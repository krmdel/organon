# Social Media Formats

Reference for the sci-communication skill's social mode. Covers thread and post formats for science communication on social platforms.

---

## Twitter/X Thread (5-8 tweets)

### Structure

1. **Hook** (1 tweet) -- Surprising finding or bold question. Must stop the scroll. Start with the most interesting part, not the background. Frame as a question, a counterintuitive fact, or a "here's what we found" statement.

2. **Context** (1-2 tweets) -- Why this matters. What was the problem or gap? Keep it relatable -- connect to something the reader already cares about.

3. **Method** (1 tweet) -- What the researchers did, simplified to one sentence. Only include if the method is interesting or novel. Skip entirely for concept-based threads.

4. **Key findings** (1-2 tweets) -- The most striking result with one specific number. Use comparisons: "2x more likely" is better than "OR = 2.1". One finding per tweet.

5. **Why it matters** (1 tweet) -- Implication for the field or society. Be specific, not vague.

6. **Caveat** (1 tweet) -- One important limitation or nuance. This is what separates good science communication from hype. Frame it constructively: "Important caveat: this was in mice, not humans yet. But the mechanism is conserved, so human trials are next."

7. **Link + CTA** (1 tweet) -- Paper link, invite discussion. "Full paper here: [link]. What do you think -- does this change how you think about [topic]?"

### Format Rules

- Each tweet: **maximum 280 characters**
- Use thread numbering: 1/7, 2/7, ... 7/7
- No hashtags in the middle of sentences -- place at end of final tweet if used at all
- **One idea per tweet** -- never split a thought across tweets
- Use line breaks within tweets for readability
- No emojis in scientific content unless the author's research profile indicates preference for them

## LinkedIn Post (single post, 1300 characters max)

### Structure

- **Opening line** -- Hook a professional audience. Lead with industry relevance or career implication. Make it personal if possible: "I just read a paper that changes how I think about [topic]."
- **2-3 short paragraphs** summarizing the finding or concept and its significance
- **One specific data point or statistic** -- concrete beats abstract
- **Closing question** to drive engagement: "What implications does this have for your work?" or "Have you seen this in practice?"

### Format Rules

- Professional but accessible tone
- Short paragraphs (1-2 sentences each)
- Line breaks between paragraphs for mobile readability
- No emojis in scientific content unless research profile indicates preference
- Do not use "I'm excited to share..." or "Thrilled to announce..." -- these are corporate-speak tells

## Bluesky Post

Similar to Twitter but allows slightly longer form:

- **300 character limit** per post
- Can thread like Twitter for longer content
- More academic audience -- can use slightly more technical language
- Same thread structure as Twitter/X but 6-7 posts typical
- Include alt-text descriptions if sharing images

## Accuracy Preservation Gate (All Social Formats)

**MANDATORY: Run this check before saving ANY social content.**

Social media's brevity creates unique accuracy risks. Check specifically for:

1. **Oversimplification that becomes false.** "Gene X causes cancer" when the paper says "Gene X is associated with increased risk in a specific subpopulation." Compression must not change meaning.

2. **Missing context that changes interpretation.** If the finding only applies to a specific population, age group, or condition, that context cannot be dropped just to fit the character limit. If it does not fit, restructure the thread to include it.

3. **Hype inflation.** Social media rewards bold claims. Resist. Check every adjective: "breakthrough" -> was it really? "First-ever" -> is that verified? "Game-changing" -> for whom, specifically?

4. **Thread coherence.** Read the thread as a stranger would -- does the hook promise something the thread delivers? Does the caveat tweet actually address the most important limitation?

5. **No unsupported extrapolation.** The thread must not speculate beyond the source material's conclusions, even if speculation would make a more engaging thread.

**If the most interesting version of a finding cannot be stated accurately in 280 characters, restructure -- do not simplify past the truth.**

## Platform Selection Guide

When the user says "social media" without specifying a platform:

| User's audience | Recommended platform(s) |
|----------------|------------------------|
| Academic peers | Twitter/X thread + Bluesky |
| Industry professionals | LinkedIn post |
| Mixed / broad reach | Twitter/X thread + LinkedIn post |
| Science-curious public | Twitter/X thread |

If unclear, generate Twitter/X thread (most versatile) and offer to adapt to other platforms.
